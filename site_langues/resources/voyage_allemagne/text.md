Our trip to Germany began and ended in exactly the same way—with music, and more specifically with “99 Luftballons” by Nena. It instantly set the tone for what turned out to be an unforgettable and truly amazing experience.

First, we had to drive to Dijon. We left at 11:30 a.m., listening to music the whole way (including our now “theme song”), and arrived at the Palais des Sports at around 1:30 p.m. From there, we continued by bus to Trier. The German and French students were separated, and the journey lasted about five and a half hours. We even passed through Luxembourg, saw a stork, and spotted a beautiful rainbow—like a perfect welcome to the adventure ahead.

When we arrived in Trier, Antonia’s father warmly welcomed us, and we still had a two-hour drive ahead to Frankenthal, Antonia’s town. We finally arrived at around 9:00 p.m. Even though we were tired, everything felt exciting. We had delicious pumpkin soup (“Lecker!” as they say in German), and I immediately felt at home. Her family is incredibly kind: she has a little sister, Matilda, who is 11 and has been learning French for a year, and a younger brother, who is 8 and absolutely adorable. He sings all the time—although instead of singing “I’m a Barbie girl in a Barbie world,” he proudly sang “I’m a baby girl in a baby world,” which made us laugh a lot.

On Sunday, after struggling a bit to find the Wi-Fi, I visited Mainz with Antonia and her mother, who speaks excellent French. We first tried to visit the cathedral, but we arrived during mass, so a man told us it would only reopen at 1 p.m. We then tried to visit a natural history museum, only to discover it had temporarily moved. So we went to the Gutenberg Museum instead and decided to focus on Johannes Gutenberg, the inventor of the printing press around 1450. I learned that he worked in Mainz and also lived in Strasbourg, which was fascinating. By then we were quite hungry, so we didn’t spend too much time on the natural history section. We had lunch at a restaurant and tried again to visit the cathedral—but once again, no luck! A priest named Joshi was being consecrated, and there were many religious people gathered. The man there even joked that it would take more than two hours “for the Holy Spirit to come down to us,” which made us laugh.

Later, we went to Antonia’s grandparents’ house by bus and train. They were incredibly welcoming, and her two cousins were there too. At first, the house seemed strangely quiet—but then we discovered the children outside, busy building a “snail hospital”! It was both funny and creative. We played UNO together before heading back home, where we enjoyed soup and pretzels.

On Monday and Tuesday, there were no classes because of the Abitur oral exams. On Monday, we went to Heidelberg with Antonia and her family. We climbed 330 steps (yes, we counted!) to reach the castle—it was enormous and impressive. We had a German-speaking guide, and although I didn’t understand everything, Antonia and her parents kindly explained a lot to me. I learned about King Frederick, who weighed over 200 kilos, and Queen Elisabeth, who had 13 children. I also discovered that princesses spoke only French at the time, and that people could even skate on the castle’s moat in winter! We saw the largest wine barrel in the world—and even climbed on top of it, which was incredible. At lunch, we shared a huge pizza, and in the afternoon I relaxed and read at home. In the evening, we played card games like Marmotte and Kems, which were so much fun.

On Tuesday, we explored Frankenthal with Antonia, her sister, and her best friend. We drank matcha tea and tried Dampfnudeln, a regional specialty. At lunch, we finished the leftover pizza (still delicious!). In the afternoon, Antonia had homework, so I spent time reading and chatting with her mother. Later, I watched Matilda’s hip-hop dance class, sitting next to another French exchange student. Funny coincidence: she was also called Mathilda—and there was yet another Mathilda in the class! That evening, we ate hamburgers and played games again. My Kems partner was Antonia’s father, and our secret sign was impossible to guess—we won almost every time!

On Wednesday, the graduating students celebrated the end of their Abitur exams. We only had two real classes out of six—and even those were mostly games. The atmosphere at school was incredible: music everywhere, students organizing games like teachers vs. students quizzes, dancing, and lots of harmless chaos. They entered classrooms, played pranks, hung lessons out of windows, built barricades with tables, and even set up tape across the hallways. Balloons were popping all day as students jumped on them. It felt like a big festival! In the afternoon, I played cards and attended Antonia’s ballet class.

The school, called Albert Einstein Gymnasium, goes from 6th grade to 13th grade (or rather 12 and a half years of schooling in that region).

On Thursday, I experienced a full “normal” school day—and honestly, it was great. Classes start later and end at 1:10 p.m., which is really nice. Each lesson lasts only 45 minutes, so it’s easier to stay focused. I had English first: the teacher was very kind, and they spoke only English during the lesson. They were even reading a book called “Torn away” —and I was really happy because I understood almost everything! Then I had German, with a topic about political leaders past and present, which I found interesting and surprisingly understandable. After that, we had two hours of math—well, math is math in any language! Then chemistry, about molecular mass. At lunchtime, we ate at a café and later attended Antonia’s singing class. In the evening, we had a cozy game night and ate ravioli with tomato sauce.

On Friday, I had a full morning of classes again: biology, physics, French, math, and German. In math, they had a test in a special classroom near the library, with lots of space between each student. After lunch at home, we went to the bowling with the family. Mathilda won the first game, and I won the second (though we all had the side barriers!). In the evening, we ate pizza, played Kems again, and watched a dance show called Let’s Dance. The TV was in the parents’ bedroom, and we were all sitting on their bed—I ended up falling a bit asleep in front of it!

On Saturday, we played cards in the morning before leaving at 11:30 with Antonia and her mother. Her father, brother, and sister had gone to a family lunch. Saying goodbye was emotional—we all had tears in our eyes and took a long time to part. Then, between Dijon and Auxerre, Clemence’s parents picked me up.

This trip was absolutely incredible. I discovered a new culture, improved my language skills, met amazing people, and created unforgettable memories. It wasn’t just a school exchange—it was a truly wonderful human experience that I will never forget.
